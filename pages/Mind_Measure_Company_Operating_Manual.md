## 1. What Mind Measure Is

Mind Measure is a wellbeing analytics platform that gives organisations their first reliable, continuous read on how their people are actually doing.

Every day, users spend one to two minutes in a natural conversation with Jodie, an AI companion. That conversation is processed using a combination of text analysis, vocal features, and facial expression signals to produce a personal Wellbeing Score on a 0 to 100 scale. Users see their own trend. The organisation sees an aggregated, anonymised picture of how the population is feeling, broken down by department, school, office, or cohort, with no access to any individual's data.

The platform runs as two products sharing a common infrastructure. **Mind Measure Pro** serves corporate employers, tracking workforce wellbeing for HR and people teams. **Mind Measure** serves universities, tracking student wellbeing across higher education institutions. Both products centre on the same core check-in experience and the same separation principle: the organisation receives population-level insight, never individual surveillance.

The platform is not a clinical tool. It does not diagnose, triage, prescribe, or replace professional mental health support. It sits upstream of those systems: it surfaces patterns early, points people toward the right support, and gives institutions the data they need to allocate resources before problems become crises.

## 2. The Problem

Most organisations have no reliable signal on how their people are doing until something has already gone wrong. The data they typically use, absence rates, retention figures, engagement survey scores, is retrospective, infrequent, and aggregated in ways that make it nearly impossible to act before a pattern becomes a crisis.

In corporate settings, the UK's Health and Safety Executive estimates that stress, depression, and anxiety accounted for 51 per cent of all work-related ill health in 2023 to 2024, and 55 per cent of all working days lost to ill health. Most employers either do not have a structured wellbeing programme, or have one that produces no usable data.

In higher education, this translates directly to financial exposure. Using HESA SB273 data and Office for Students Condition B3 analysis, Mind Measure's working estimate of the tuition income at risk across the UK higher education sector from student mental health challenges is approximately £600 million annually. Universities have a regulatory obligation under OfS Condition B3 to demonstrate they are supporting student outcomes. The sector has the intent, but generally not the infrastructure, to act early.

The market timing is favourable. Since the Covid-19 pandemic, board-level attention to workforce wellbeing has increased substantially. NICE guidelines on mental health in the workplace, OfS regulatory requirements for universities, and the general shift toward preventive health spending have all improved the receptiveness of institutional procurement teams. Simultaneously, the technology to do this affordably at scale — multimodal AI analysis that runs in real time on consumer devices — has only recently become viable.

## 4. The Products

### 4.1 Mind Measure Pro (Corporate)

The employee-facing product comprises a native iOS and Android app and a web-based employer dashboard, with a Content Hub and Conversational Insight Hub as additional modules.

Employees complete a one-off baseline assessment of five to ten minutes on first use. Jodie walks them through the PHQ-2 (a validated two-question depression screening instrument) and the GAD-2 (a validated two-question anxiety screening instrument), alongside a self-reported mood rating and open conversational prompts. This establishes their personal baseline, the reference point against which all future daily check-ins are measured.

From that point, employees are encouraged to check in daily. Each check-in is a natural one-to-two-minute conversation with Jodie. There are no forms, scales, or multiple-choice questions. After the conversation, the platform produces the employee's Mind Measure Score: a 0 to 100 value reflecting their overall wellbeing, displayed with a colour-coded band (Excellent: 80 to 100, Good: 60 to 79, Moderate: 45 to 59, Concerning: 0 to 44). Employees see their score, their mood trend, a summary of what they discussed, and their check-in streak.

The employer dashboard gives HR and people teams a real-time aggregated view of how the workforce is doing. Key features include the overview with live pulse metrics, a "Where to look" panel that ranks departments and offices by wellbeing band, cohort analysis by department, office, seniority, and work mode, a content management system for publishing wellbeing articles and targeted nudge notifications, an in-app alerts system for time-sensitive company-wide messages, and the Conversational Insight Hub, a feature that allows organisations to pose natural-language questions woven into employees' check-ins and read anonymised themes from the responses.

At no point can an employer see an individual employee's score, conversation, or identifying information. The minimum cohort size for any aggregated view is five.

### 4.2 Mind Measure (University)

The structure mirrors the corporate product, with a student-facing mobile app and a web-based institutional dashboard. The differences are in terminology, configuration, and the organisational hierarchy (schools, departments, year groups, and halls of residence rather than business units, offices, and seniority bands).

The university dashboard gives wellbeing, student services, and professional teams a real-time aggregated view of how the student population is doing. Key features include the overview with live pulse metrics, cohort analysis by school, department, year group, halls of residence, and study mode, a content management system for publishing wellbeing articles and short nudge notifications targeted to specific cohorts, an in-app alerts system for time-sensitive institution-wide messages, and the Conversational Insight Hub, a paid add-on that allows universities to pose natural-language questions woven into students' check-ins and read anonymised themes from the responses.

At no point can a university see an individual student's score, conversation, or identifying information. The minimum cohort size for any aggregated view is five.

### 4.3 Features Common to Both Products

Both products share the following, delivered through the common backend infrastructure.

The **Conversational Insight Hub** is the highest-engagement paid-tier feature. It allows institutional administrators to write a natural-language question, target it to a specific cohort, and have Jodie weave it into matching users' check-ins over a defined period. Results are presented as anonymised themes, sentiment splits, and paraphrased quotes. The hub shows cohort divergence across demographic groups and can produce a structured PDF cohort insight report. It replaces traditional pulse surveys with a mechanism that generates richer data because it sits inside a conversation rather than a form. Question delivery is now fully live end-to-end on both products: the student mobile check-in resolves cohort-matched questions and hands them to Jodie in the same way the corporate product does. The feature is delivered through two standalone Conversational Insight hubs (one per product line) that sign in by handoff from the admin dashboards and proxy to the core insight endpoints.

The **Alerts system** allows administrators to send time-sensitive, severity-tagged messages to all users or specific cohorts. Messages render as a prominent card on the user's dashboard within seconds and trigger push notifications at higher severity levels. Four tiers cover Urgent (campus closure, evacuation), Welfare (supportive message after difficult news), Notice (schedule changes), and Info (events, announcements). A built-in safeguard prevents any alert being sent to fewer than five users.

The **Content Hub** allows institutions to publish wellbeing articles and short nudges, import content from a shared pool, and target content to specific cohorts using cascading demographic filters. It surfaces what users are currently talking about as editorial guidance.

The **Weekly Reflection** is a personalised editorial email sent each Sunday (08:00 UTC), summarising the user's week through the lens of their Jodie conversations, with an AI-generated narrative, a score trend chart, discussion highlights, and curated resource links. It now runs on both products from the same pipeline, with a deliberate difference in consent posture. On the university product it is opt-out: students receive it by default and can mute it from the app. On the corporate product it is opt-in and doubly gated: the employer must be entitled (a Mind Measure superuser enables it per company) and the employee must opt in from the app, so nothing is sent until both are true. The Pro version carries no employer-disclosure path: welfare weeks surface the organisation's own support (EAP, Mental Health First Aiders) rather than university-style peer or institutional routing, and the email is branded in the Pro palette. The same per-send safety holds apply on both products (see section 8). This is separate from the on-demand institutional weekly summary report for HR teams, which is a different report type.

## 5. The Methodology

### 5.1 How the Score Is Produced

The Mind Measure Score is produced by a multimodal fusion engine that analyses three signals from each check-in conversation.

**Linguistic analysis (70 per cent weight)** is the primary signal. AWS Bedrock, running Claude 3.7 Sonnet, processes the conversation transcript and returns a structured output including a mood score, PHQ-2 and GAD-2 clinical indicators, risk markers, sentiment polarity, and key themes (positives, negatives, salient topics). The text analysis is the most clinically grounded signal because it captures what the person actually said.

**Vocal analysis (15 per cent weight)** extracts speech features using the Web Audio API, processing in real time on the user's device. The features analysed include mean pitch, speaking rate, jitter (pitch variation), shimmer (amplitude variation), pause patterns, energy level, and vocal quality. Research links these acoustic characteristics to emotional state and psychological wellbeing; lower pitch variability, slower speaking rate, and higher jitter and shimmer are associated with depression and low mood.

**Visual analysis (15 per cent weight)** uses AWS Rekognition to analyse the user's facial expression during the check-in when the camera is on. Features include smile frequency, eye contact, blink rate, facial affect (positive to negative), and facial tension. These complement the linguistic and vocal signals.

The fusion algorithm combines the three modality scores into a single value. If one modality is unavailable, the weights redistribute automatically: if visual is absent, text takes 80 per cent and audio takes 20 per cent; if both audio and visual are absent, the score is derived from text alone. This graceful degradation ensures the platform works even in low-quality technical environments.

A sanity floor is applied to prevent conservative audio or visual scores pulling down a clearly positive check-in: where the text score is at or above 75, no risk is detected, and overall signal quality is reasonable, the final score will not fall below 60.

Baseline assessments use a different weight profile: 70 per cent clinical (PHQ-2, GAD-2, and self-reported mood), 15 per cent audio, and 15 per cent visual. The baseline is more clinically anchored because it includes validated structured instruments.

### 5.2 Research Foundation

The PHQ-2 and GAD-2 are internationally validated screening instruments recommended by NICE and the American Psychiatric Association for initial mental health screening. Each uses two questions scored 0 to 6; a score of 3 or above on either instrument indicates a positive screen requiring follow-up.

The vocal and visual feature analysis is grounded in peer-reviewed research, including studies linking vocal characteristics (pitch, rate, jitter) to depression and anxiety, facial action coding system research on emotional state, and linguistic marker studies on the relationship between word choice and psychological distress. These signals are research-informed but have not yet undergone independent clinical validation as standalone diagnostic instruments.

### 5.3 Honest Limitations

The platform is transparent about what it does not do. The Mind Measure Score is not a clinical diagnosis. It is a wellbeing indicator to be read alongside professional judgement, not instead of it. Device quality, ambient noise, lighting, and connection stability can affect analysis accuracy. The scoring weights represent calibrated initial values that will be refined through longitudinal validation. A student or employee who disengages from the platform generates no signal.

Research priorities for the next phase include longitudinal validation against established clinical measures (PHQ-9 and GAD-7), population norms across different demographics, algorithmic fairness and bias auditing, and refinement of fusion weights based on outcome data.

## 6. Technology and Infrastructure

### 6.1 Architecture Overview

Mind Measure is built on a cloud-native AWS architecture. All services run in eu-west-2 (London), ensuring UK data residency throughout.

The platform comprises nine production web applications sharing a common backend: the university admin dashboard, the Pro admin dashboard, the student mobile app, the employee mobile app, the university Content Hub, the corporate Content Hub, the university and corporate Conversational Insight hubs, and a marketing CMS. The student and employee apps are built with React, TypeScript, and Vite, wrapped in Capacitor for native iOS and Android distribution; both are live on the App Store and Google Play. The two Conversational Insight hubs are thin Next.js front ends that proxy to the core insight endpoints and hold no business logic of their own.

Backend processing runs on six AWS Lambda functions for session processing (check-in analysis, score calculation, database writes), with Aurora Serverless v2 PostgreSQL as the primary database, Cognito for authentication, S3 for encrypted file storage, Bedrock for text analysis, Rekognition for visual analysis, SQS for asynchronous session finalisation, SES for transactional email, KMS for key management, and Secrets Manager for credential storage.

Applications are hosted on Vercel (Pro plan) with automated deployment from GitHub, TLS 1.3 enforced at the edge, and Vercel cron for scheduled jobs.

### 6.2 The Multimodal Fusion Engine as IP

The core intellectual property is the multimodal fusion engine: the combination of real-time voice AI, audio feature extraction, facial expression analysis, and clinical indicator scoring (PHQ-2 and GAD-2) within a single two-minute conversational check-in, with graceful degradation across modalities and a calibrated fusion algorithm. This architecture is non-trivial to replicate. It requires expertise across conversational AI, clinical assessment methodology, audio signal processing, computer vision, and backend infrastructure, integrated into a consumer mobile experience that users complete daily.

### 6.3 Code Quality and Maintainability

All frontend applications share a consistent stack: React 19, TypeScript, and Vite. TypeScript is configured at maximum strictness, including exactOptionalPropertyTypes and noUncheckedIndexedAccess — two settings that most production codebases do not enable. This produces self-documenting code and eliminates an entire category of runtime bugs at compile time.

Approximately 15,800 lines of code that were duplicated between the university and Pro products have been consolidated into four private NPM packages: api-lib (shared API middleware), shared-services (security services, scoring engine, and utilities), shared-types (TypeScript interfaces), and shared-ui (48 shared UI components). The repositories remain independent, which preserves M&A flexibility: any repository can be transferred or sold independently without restructuring.

The platform has 750-plus unit tests with enforced coverage thresholds in CI; any pull request that reduces coverage below baseline is automatically blocked. End-to-end tests use Playwright across eight specification files, including visual regression testing for the baseline assessment. Pre-commit hooks enforce Prettier formatting and ESLint on every commit.

### 6.4 Monitoring and Observability

An automated ecosystem health audit runs every four hours via a Vercel cron job. It checks database connectivity and latency, API endpoint availability, authentication system status, environment variable configuration, cost tracking, and voice agent activity. Results are evaluated automatically: critical issues or a score drop of more than 15 points trigger an immediate email alert via SES; warning-only results produce a daily digest. Sentry error tracking is integrated across all four active applications, covering both client-side and server-side errors with session replay on error and ErrorBoundary wrapping at the app root. CloudWatch and CloudTrail provide infrastructure-level logging with one-year and seven-year retention respectively.

## 7. Security and Compliance

### 7.1 Security Architecture

Data at rest is encrypted with AES-256 at the storage layer using AWS KMS-managed keys (Aurora storage encryption and S3). Data in transit is encrypted with TLS 1.3 from browser to Vercel edge and TLS 1.2 or above from Vercel to Aurora. Application-level, per-field encryption of the most sensitive columns (transcripts, assessment results) is designed as defence in depth but is not yet enforced on the server-side write path: the back-office analysis pipeline reads these in plaintext, so they are stored in plaintext within the encrypted volume. Implementing it on that safe subset is tracked as a security follow-up.

Authentication uses AWS Cognito with RS256 JWT tokens. All API routes verify JWT signatures cryptographically against the Cognito JWKS endpoint. Multi-factor authentication is supported via TOTP and SMS. Superuser access uses a separate flow with time-limited HMAC-hashed 6-digit codes sent to verified Mind Measure email addresses.

Access control is role-based. The roles for university and corporate deployments include full admin access, wellbeing lead (analytics, content, nudges, and alerts), department-level read-only access, and content-only or insight-only access. Data scoping ensures institution administrators can only access data within their own organisation.

Rate limiting uses a four-tier sliding-window middleware: authentication endpoints at 8 requests per 60 seconds, general API endpoints at 60, AI endpoints at 12, and upload endpoints at 10. Standard IETF rate-limit headers are returned on all responses. This sits on top of Vercel's built-in DDoS protection.

Audit logging is two-tier: API-level fire-and-forget logging and service-level database-backed logging with HIPAA and GDPR compliance flagging. Audit logs are stored in an immutable database table protected by triggers that prevent modification or deletion. Retention is seven years.

### 7.2 Compliance Posture

The platform is designed to meet the requirements of UK GDPR, HIPAA, and SOC 2 Type II.

For UK GDPR, the right to erasure (Article 17) is fully implemented: account deletion clears all 12 user tables, anonymises audit logs, and deletes the Cognito account. The right of access (Article 15) is implemented via comprehensive report generation with email delivery and secure token access. Data subject requests are tracked in a dedicated table with SLA monitoring. All AWS services are in eu-west-2, maintaining UK data residency.

For HIPAA, administrative safeguards (RBAC, audit logging, incident tracking), technical safeguards (AES-256 encryption, JWT verification, access controls), and organisational safeguards (VPC isolation for Lambda functions, IAM least privilege, Secrets Manager credential management) are implemented and documented.

The platform's current status is that the technical controls are enterprise-grade. The gap is in formal governance documentation and audit evidence trails required for certification. Cyber Essentials Plus has been identified as the immediate certification priority, given its relevance to UK university and public sector procurement. ISO 27001 mapping follows.

### 7.3 Data Minimisation and the Separation Principle

Audio is processed for analysis and deleted within 24 hours. Still images from facial analysis are deleted immediately after processing. The platform does not collect precise location data, contact lists, photographs, other app usage, or browsing history. Conversation transcripts are retained for the duration of participation plus 12 months.

The separation principle — that institutions receive only anonymised population-level data and never individual scores, conversations, or identities — is architectural, not just a policy. The minimum cohort size of five is enforced at the query level. An institution cannot configure the platform to reveal individual data, because the queries that would enable it do not exist.

## 8. The Wellbeing Protection Policy

Mind Measure operates tiered response frameworks that define how the platform acts when a user's data indicates they may be at risk. The student and corporate employee models are distinct in their legal context, support infrastructure, and escalation pathways. They are described separately below.

### 8.1 Student Model

Universities do not currently owe a legally enforced duty of care to students in respect of mental health — the UK legal position remains unsettled following *Abrahart v University of Bristol* (2024) — but the moral and reputational expectation is clear. The UUK Suicide-Safer Universities framework and OfS effective practice guidance set the standard the sector is judged against. Mind Measure's student framework is designed to be consistent with both.

The student model has six response levels. Levels 0 to 2 (week averages of 50 and above, or a single harder week between 40 and 49) are handled entirely within the product. The student receives a Weekly Reflection with an appropriate tone; no escalation occurs.

Level 3 (every check-in in a week below 40, with at least three check-ins completed) is a welfare week. The student receives a welfare-modified reflection that directly acknowledges a difficult week and surfaces support in proximity order: their Buddy (a peer accountability feature built into the student app), the university's wellbeing team, and sector-level services. The university is not notified at Level 3. This is a student having a genuinely difficult week; automatic identification at this level would be disproportionate and would overwhelm welfare teams.

**What is currently live:** Per-check-in safety checks run before every Weekly Reflection send. These hold the email and alert Mind Measure staff if a single-week score falls below 25, a Bedrock safety flag is raised, crisis keywords are detected, or a welfare week is identified. The database schema for compound trigger tracking, the safeguarding-contact admin UI, a read-only Welfare Record panel for each student, and a welfare timeline with PDF export are also in place.

**What is not yet built:** The cross-week compound trigger engine (`evaluateCompoundTriggers()`) and the automated Level 4 disclosure pipeline (`sendSafeguardingDisclosure()`) do not exist. The tables that would record trigger state and disclosure notifications are created but never written to. No university safeguarding contact is notified automatically today.

Level 4, as specified, is a safeguarding disclosure triggered when any one of four compound criteria is met: a week average below 25 for two consecutive weeks; two or more crisis-language flags within a 7-day window; a score drop of 40 or more points against the trailing 4-week average; or three or more welfare weeks in any 6-week rolling window. This logic is designed and the schema exists, but the build is blocked on clinical advisor sign-off on the trigger thresholds and legal review of the DPA addendum and notification pathway.

Level 5 covers real-time crisis response during an active check-in. This is not yet built and is a critical priority.

Students consent to the Level 4 and 5 disclosure pathway at account creation: a dedicated consent screen explains exactly what triggers disclosure, what is shared, and who receives it. The legal basis is UK GDPR Article 9(2)(a) (explicit consent), with substantial public interest under DPA 2018 Schedule 1, paragraph 18 as a secondary basis.

### 8.2 Corporate Employee Model

The corporate context is legally and structurally different. Employers have established duties under the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 to take reasonable steps to prevent work-related stress and mental ill health. The Equality Act 2010 imposes anticipatory adjustment duties. These create a clearer legal basis for employer action than universities currently have, but they also create different expectations about what that action looks like.

Crucially, the corporate product does not include a Buddy system. Employees are not paired with peers for wellbeing accountability in the same way students are. The support chain at Level 3 routes to the organisation's existing wellbeing infrastructure instead: the Employee Assistance Programme, Mental Health First Aiders, and the HR or People team, as configured during onboarding.

The tiered response framework for employees mirrors the structural logic of the student model. Levels 0 to 2 are handled within the product. Level 3 (a welfare week) surfaces the configured organisational support resources without notifying the employer. Level 4 (a compound trigger disclosure) sends an identified notification to the organisation's designated welfare lead, using the same four compound criteria as the student model. Level 5 real-time crisis response is handled differently on the corporate product: an in-app crisis interstitial is already live on the employee mobile app, surfacing emergency resources immediately if crisis-level content is detected during a check-in. This is ahead of the university product on this specific point.

The opt-in Pro Weekly Reflection is the live vehicle for Levels 0 to 3 on the corporate product. It runs the same per-send safety holds as the university pipeline: before any reflection is sent, a per-check-in safety check holds the email and alerts Mind Measure staff if a single-week score falls below 25, a Bedrock safety flag is raised, crisis keywords are detected, or a welfare week is identified. A welfare week produces a welfare-modified reflection that routes the employee to the organisation's own support (the EAP, Mental Health First Aiders, and the company's wellbeing portal, configured during onboarding) and to sector-level services such as Samaritans, never to the employer. As with the university product, the cross-week compound trigger engine and any Level 4 pathway are not yet built; the Pro product additionally has no employer-disclosure path by design.

Level 4 employer disclosure does not exist on the corporate product by design. The decision not to build an employer-facing compound trigger disclosure for employees reflects the different legal and trust context: employment relationships make automated employer notification of individual employee distress a substantially higher-risk mechanism than university safeguarding disclosure. The corporate product's welfare response focuses on surfacing the right organisational resources to the employee at Levels 2 and 3, rather than escalating to the employer.

The compound trigger design on the student model is deliberate: a single bad week, a single flag, a single drop does not trigger disclosure. The triggers are calibrated to catch sustained or severe concern.

Employees consent to the data processing framework at account creation under the same GDPR basis as students.

## 9. Commercial Model

### 9.1 Pricing

The university product is priced at £4 per student per year for standard access, with additional fees for the Conversational Insight Hub add-on.

Mind Measure Pro is priced with a one-off setup fee ($15,000 to $25,000) and a per-employee ladder: $36 per head for organisations of 1,000 to 5,000 employees, $30 for 5,000 to 10,000, and $26 for 10,000 to 20,000. The Conversational Insight Hub is included at standard pricing and may be waived as a concession for named launch partners.

These prices are positioned at the top of the market, intentionally. The first marquee contract sets the reference rate for all subsequent contracts. Pricing below market risks a perception problem with sophisticated institutional buyers.

### 9.2 Sales Motion

The onboarding process begins with a company onboarding questionnaire covering organisational structure, existing wellbeing support, safeguarding contacts, IT and SSO requirements, and data processing preferences. This takes approximately 30 minutes for the client and produces the configuration inputs for a fully provisioned deployment. The platform does not require integration with existing institutional systems: users register through the app using their institutional email address, and the platform associates them automatically with their organisation.

### 9.3 Commercial Strategy

Mind Measure's commercial approach prioritises proving the model with a small number of paying institutional clients before pursuing aggressive growth. This is enabled by low running costs (under £1,000 per month at current scale), which mean the business is not dependent on external capital to operate or demonstrate value.

Mind Measure is eligible for the UK Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme (SEIS), confirmed via SeedLegals. When external investment is sought, SEIS provides meaningful tax reliefs for UK-based angel investors.

## 11. Roadmap

### 11.1 Immediate Priorities

On the product side, the principal near-term engineering priority is building the compound trigger engine and automated Level 4 notification pipeline for the university safeguarding disclosure pathway. This is blocked on process rather than engineering: clinical advisor sign-off on trigger thresholds, a legal review of the DPA addendum and notification pathway, and privacy policy updates are the prerequisites. None of these is a long engineering task once sign-off is obtained. (Conversational Insight v2 reached university parity in June 2026: the student mobile check-in now resolves and delivers cohort-matched questions exactly as the corporate product does, so that item is closed.)

On compliance, Cyber Essentials Plus certification is the immediate priority, followed by ISO 27001 Annex A mapping.

### 11.2 Medium-Term Development

The roadmap items currently in active planning include: the cohort template library (converting cohort analysis from a per-session build-it-yourself tool into a shared institutional library of live-data templates); the student ambassador platform (a social assets library and referral tracking system for organic user acquisition); and the score reveal interaction (a short transition between processing completion and score display that increases the perceived significance of the result).

At medium priority, load testing under concurrent usage is planned, alongside Jodie prompt improvements (adding named event capture, sleep and energy probes, positive anchors, and social connection probes to the weekly reflection source data).

### 11.3 Longer-Term Opportunities

The roadmap identifies several longer-term opportunities that are captured but not yet in active development: a text-based check-in alternative for users who prefer not to use voice, multi-language support for international student populations, a peer support network evolving from the current buddy system, and an API layer for institutional systems integration.

Voice provider abstraction is a low-priority R&D item at current scale, with NVIDIA PersonaPlex identified as a viable self-hosted alternative that breaks even at approximately 10,000 check-in minutes per month.

## 12. Competitive Landscape

### 12.1 Direct Competitors

**Unmind** is the most directly comparable enterprise wellbeing platform in the UK. It offers wellbeing content, assessments, and manager training, primarily targeting large employers. It does not offer a conversational AI check-in or multimodal analysis. Its data model is based on self-reported questionnaires and app usage.

**Headspace for Work** is the major US-based competitor in corporate wellbeing, focused on mindfulness content and EAP integration. Its population analytics are limited and its check-in mechanism is a simple mood slider.

Neither competitor offers a conversational AI check-in, multimodal scoring (text, audio, visual), or the same separation principle architecture. The student wellbeing segment is less crowded; most university-focused tools offer questionnaire-based screening or static resource platforms, not continuous conversational monitoring.

### 12.2 Competitive Moat

Mind Measure's durable advantages are three: the multimodal fusion engine (technically non-trivial to replicate), the privacy-by-architecture approach (a structural trust signal that competitors offering surveillance-based tools cannot match), and the daily conversational format (which generates a richer, higher-frequency data stream than questionnaire-based alternatives, producing stronger longitudinal insight as usage scales).

The ElevenLabs dependency is an acknowledged risk. The platform's architecture was designed with this in mind: conversation data, transcripts, scores, and analysis are completely platform-agnostic. Only the real-time voice layer depends on ElevenLabs. The planned provider abstraction layer addresses lock-in risk.

## 13. Key Risks and Mitigations

**Bus factor.** The company is currently founder-operated. Mitigation: comprehensive documentation (this manual and the full docs site), consistent technology patterns across all products, enforced testing standards, and an experienced Non-Executive Director with commercial relationships.

**Clinical credibility.** The multimodal scoring weights have not yet been independently validated. Mitigation: validated clinical instruments (PHQ-2, GAD-2) form the baseline; the platform is positioned consistently as a monitoring tool, not a diagnostic one; longitudinal validation is a stated research priority.

**Safeguarding liability.** The automated Level 4 notification pipeline introduces legal and reputational risk if triggered incorrectly. Mitigation: compound trigger design that requires sustained or severe concern before disclosure; immutable audit trail on every notification; post-notification review queue; clinical and legal sign-off required before the automated pipeline goes live; GDPR consent model documented and peer-reviewed against the Northumbria University OfS precedent.

**Voice provider dependency.** ElevenLabs is the primary voice AI provider, with approximately 35 production files touching the integration. Mitigation: provider abstraction layer on the roadmap; conversation data is provider-agnostic; NVIDIA PersonaPlex identified as a viable self-hosted alternative.

**Regulatory change.** The UK legal position on universities' duty of care to students is unsettled following Abrahart. Mind Measure does not position itself as a duty-of-care instrument; it positions as an early-awareness tool with a defined safeguarding escalation pathway consistent with UUK and OfS guidance.

**Scaling voice costs.** ElevenLabs costs approximately $0.10 per minute. At significant scale this becomes a material cost line. Mitigation: provider abstraction and PersonaPlex research; cost is modelled into pricing; break-even on self-hosting is approximately 10,000 minutes per month.

## 14. Summary

Mind Measure is a technically differentiated, privacy-first wellbeing analytics platform addressing a genuine and growing institutional need in two large markets. The core intellectual property, a multimodal fusion engine combining conversational AI, vocal analysis, and facial expression analysis in a daily check-in, is non-trivial to replicate. The architecture is enterprise-grade, the testing and code quality standards are high, and the operational documentation is thorough.

The platform is live on both the App Store and Google Play, running costs are under £1,000 per month, and the commercial strategy is to prove the model with a small number of paying institutional clients before pursuing external investment.

The clearest risks, clinical validation, founder dependence, and the automated safeguarding pipeline, are acknowledged and being addressed systematically. None of them is a structural weakness; all of them are the ordinary work of building a company from this stage.
