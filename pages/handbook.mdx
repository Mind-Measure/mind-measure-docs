# Part A: Understanding Mind Measure

## 1. About Mind Measure

Mind Measure is a multimodal conversational analytics platform built to support student wellbeing in higher education. Through a brief daily conversation with an AI companion called Jodie, students receive a personalised wellbeing score that tracks how they are doing over time, giving both the individual and their institution a clearer picture of how they are feeling.

The platform is **not** a diagnostic tool, a replacement for counselling, or a clinical intervention. It is a structured, evidence-informed monitoring system that helps universities notice emerging patterns, allocate resources more effectively, and support students before small concerns become serious problems.

### What Mind Measure Is

- A daily wellness check-in that takes one to two minutes
- A personal baseline assessment that establishes an individual's starting point
- A dashboard for students to track their own wellbeing
- An institutional analytics platform for aggregated, anonymised insights
- A content hub for universities to share wellbeing resources and nudges

### What Mind Measure Is Not

- A medical device or clinical diagnostic tool
- A replacement for professional mental health support
- A surveillance tool: institutions never see individual conversations
- An app that collects location data, contact lists, photos, or browsing history

## 2. Our Philosophy

Mind Measure operates on a simple principle: **early awareness leads to better outcomes**. Most students who struggle with their mental health do not seek help until they are in crisis. By providing a low-friction, non-judgemental way to reflect on how they are feeling each day, we aim to normalise the conversation around mental health and create a continuous feedback loop between students and the support systems around them.

Our approach is grounded in three commitments:

**Privacy first.** Students own their data. Individual conversations and scores are never shared with universities. Institutions receive only aggregated, anonymised analytics at cohort level.

**Evidence-informed.** Our methodology draws on validated clinical screening instruments (PHQ-2, GAD-2) and peer-reviewed research into vocal biomarkers, facial expression analysis, and linguistic markers of wellbeing.

**Human-centred design.** Technology serves the conversation, not the other way around. Jodie's conversational style is warm, non-clinical, and adaptive. The experience is designed to feel like a brief check-in with a trusted companion, not a form to fill in.

## 3. What your students do and see

Your students use a mobile app. You never see their individual conversations or scores; this section is simply so you understand their experience.

### Their first time: the baseline

The first time a student opens the app, they complete a one-off baseline assessment lasting five to ten minutes. Jodie asks a series of validated clinical screening questions alongside more open-ended prompts. This establishes the student's personal wellness baseline, the reference point against which all future check-ins are measured.

The clinical screening includes:

- **PHQ-2**: a two-question depression screening tool widely used in primary care (scored 0 to 6)
- **GAD-2**: a two-question generalised anxiety screening tool (scored 0 to 6)
- **Self-reported mood**: a single 1 to 10 scale question

These instruments are internationally validated and recommended by NICE and the American Psychiatric Association for initial screening.

### Their daily rhythm: the check-in

After the baseline, students are encouraged to check in daily. Each check-in is a brief one-to-two-minute conversation with Jodie, who asks how they are feeling, follows up on themes from previous conversations, and gently explores what is going well and what might be troubling them. There are no forms, scales, or multiple-choice questions; students simply talk.

### What they see afterwards

After each check-in the platform computes a **Mind Measure Score** on a 0 to 100 scale, reflecting the student's overall wellbeing based on a multimodal analysis of their conversation. It is shown on their personal dashboard with a wellbeing band:

| Range | Label | Dashboard colour |
|---|---|---|
| 80 to 100 | Excellent | Lilac |
| 60 to 79 | Good | Teal |
| 40 to 59 | Building momentum | Amber |
| 0 to 39 | Support available | Coral |

Alongside the score, students see their mood trend, their check-in streak, short summaries of what they have been talking about, and a comparison with their previous check-in. They also receive the **nudges** and **alerts** you send, and they can generate their own downloadable **wellbeing report** covering their recent history.

## 4. The science behind the score

Mind Measure combines three complementary signals from each conversation:

**Linguistic analysis**: what students say, and how they say it. The platform analyses sentiment, word choice, engagement, coherence, and the balance of positive and negative language. Patterns associated with depression, anxiety, and stress are identified using natural language processing.

**Vocal analysis**: how students sound. Research links vocal characteristics including pitch variability, speaking rate, pause patterns, voice energy, and vocal quality to emotional state and wellbeing. These features are extracted and analysed in real time.

**Visual analysis**: how students present, where the camera is on. Facial expression analysis examines indicators such as smile frequency, eye contact, emotional valence, and overall engagement. These signals complement the linguistic and vocal data.

A fusion algorithm combines these into the single score, weighting the most reliable and clinically relevant signals and coping gracefully when one is missing (for example, when the camera is off).

### Research foundation

The assessment methodology draws on established research in:

- **Clinical screening**: PHQ-2 and GAD-2 are validated screening instruments with extensive evidence bases
- **Vocal biomarkers**: peer-reviewed studies linking vocal characteristics to depression, anxiety, and stress
- **Facial expression analysis**: research on facial action coding systems and their relationship to emotional state
- **Linguistic markers**: studies on language patterns associated with psychological distress

### Honest limitations

- The Mind Measure Score is **not a clinical diagnosis**. It is a wellbeing indicator to be read alongside professional judgement.
- Audio and visual features are research-informed but have not yet undergone independent clinical validation as standalone diagnostic instruments.
- Technical factors (device quality, internet connection, ambient noise, lighting) can affect the quality of multimodal analysis.
- The platform needs regular use to build a meaningful picture; isolated check-ins should be read with caution.
- Fusion algorithm weights represent initial calibration and will be refined through ongoing longitudinal validation.

### Ongoing research

Mind Measure is committed to continuous validation and improvement. Research priorities include longitudinal validation against established clinical measures (PHQ-9, GAD-7), population norms across different demographics and institutions, algorithmic fairness and bias auditing, and refinement of scoring weights based on outcome data.

## 5. Privacy and the separation principle

Mind Measure is deliberately designed so that an institution cannot identify, monitor, or single out an individual student from their wellbeing data. This is the principle that makes the platform a benefit rather than surveillance.

- **You never see individual conversations or individual scores.** Everything you see is aggregated.
- **A minimum group size protects anonymity.** Aggregated views and conversational insight only reveal results once a group is large enough (at least five responses) that no individual can be inferred.
- **Sensitive data is minimised and protected.** Audio is processed for analysis and not retained as recordings; data is encrypted in transit (TLS 1.3) and at rest (AES-256); administrative access requires multi-factor authentication.
- **Students hold rights over their data** under UK GDPR, including access, rectification, erasure, and portability.

# Part B: Using Mind Measure, step-by-step

The guides below describe the tasks your team performs. On-screen labels are shown in **bold** so you can follow along in the product. ## 6. Reading your dashboard

The university dashboard is your day-to-day view of how the student population is doing. It updates automatically (roughly once a minute) and never shows individual students.

<img src="/images/handbook/uni-dashboard-overview.png" alt="The university Overview dashboard with the headline wellbeing score, live pulse metrics, the Where to look panel, the schools grid, and the trend and distribution charts" style={{ width: '100%', borderRadius: '8px', margin: '1rem 0' }} />

*The Overview at a glance: the headline score and band, the live pulse, the score and mood trend, the distribution donut, Where to look, and the per-school breakdown.*

### Getting oriented

1. Sign in and you land on the **Overview**. The left sidebar offers **Overview**, **Cohort Analysis**, **Saved Dashboard**, and **CMS**.
2. At the top, set the time window with **7 days**, **30 days**, or **90 days**. Every metric on the page responds to this.
3. Use **Display** to switch to a large-screen "wall" layout (ideal for a team-room screen) and back to **Standard**.
4. **Export** downloads the current view; **Refresh** pulls the latest figures; **Logout** signs you out.

### What each section tells you

Working down the Overview:

- **Live pulse**: **Active today**, **Check-ins today**, **Completion rate**, and **Avg mood**, updating as students check in.
- **Headline metrics**: **Mind Measure Score** (with its week-on-week change), **Self-Reported Mood**, **Active Today**, **Avg Streak**, **Completion Rate**, and **Response Quality**.
- **7 Day Score & Mood Trend**: the score and self-reported mood plotted together over the window, with an **AI Q&A** option to ask plain-language questions of the data.
- **Distribution**: a donut showing how the population splits across **Excellent**, **Good**, **Moderate**, and **Concerning**.
- **Where to look**: the most important panel for action. It flags the schools or halls that need attention by wellbeing band and participation, and highlights a group **doing well**, with the change versus the previous period.
- **Schools**: every school with its current score, the change **vs last week**, its standing **vs University Avg**, and its participation rate. **View all schools** expands the list.
- **Positive Themes** and **Student Concerns**: what students are talking about, with concerns tagged **High**, **Medium**, or **Low** by how widespread they are.
- **Halls of Residence**: top-performing accommodation by wellbeing score.
- **Engagement Metrics**: **Daily Check-ins**, **Median Streak**, **Completion Rate**, and **Response Quality**.
- **Conversational Insight**: a summary of your live questions (questions live, responses, response rate, overall sentiment) with a **Manage** link through to the Insight Hub (see [section 11](#11-the-conversational-insight-hub)).

### Going deeper

Many cards are clickable. Selecting **Positive Themes** or **Student Concerns** opens a detail view that can **Generate AI Insights** summarising the pattern and suggesting responses. Clicking a school in **Where to look** or the **Schools** grid opens a breakdown by department, year group, and study mode.

**Reading the score bands:** Excellent (80+), Good (60 to 79), Moderate (45 to 59), Concerning (below 45). Treat **Where to look** and the **Student Concerns** list as your weekly action prompts, not the headline average.

## 7. The Content Hub

The Content Hub is where you publish wellbeing content to your students. It opens from your institutional dashboard with seamless authentication (no separate login) and lives at its own address. Its sidebar offers **Home**, **Your Articles**, **Content Pool**, **Nudges**, and **Student Insights**.

<img src="/images/handbook/uni-content-hub.png" alt="The university Content Hub home page with curated student-welfare news, the latest published articles, and a summary of what students are saying" style={{ width: '100%', borderRadius: '8px', margin: '1rem 0' }} />

*The Content Hub home: curated sector news, your latest articles, and an at-a-glance read of what students are talking about (Top Concerns and Top Interests, with a Rummidge versus National toggle) to guide what you write next.*

### Writing and publishing an article

1. Select **Write Article** (or go to **Your Articles** and select **New Article**).
2. Set the **Audience**: **Whole University** (default) or **Specific Cohort** (see [targeting](#targeting-content-to-a-cohort)).
3. Optionally turn on **Pin to top** to keep time-sensitive content at the top of the feed.
4. Fill in **Title**, **Subtitle**, **Author**, and **Publish Date**. Title and author are required to publish.
5. Add a **Cover Image** by URL or with **Attach**.
6. Choose **Categories** and **Tags**.
7. Optionally enable **Share to Content Pool** to make the article available to other institutions.
8. Write the article in the **Content** editor; the right-hand **Preview** shows exactly how it will appear.
9. Select **Save Draft** to hold it, or **Publish** to send it live.

*The article editor: set the audience, fill in the details, choose categories and tags, and write the body, while the right-hand Preview shows how it will look to students.*

### Using the Content Pool

The **Content Pool** is a library of articles shared by Mind Measure and other institutions.

1. Open **Content Pool** and use the search box or category pills (Relationships, Wellbeing, Self-Care, Anxiety, In The News, Exam Stress, Resilience) to browse.
2. Select **Import** on any article; it is copied into your library as a **draft**.
3. Go to **Your Articles**, open the imported draft, personalise it for your students, and publish. A banner reminds you to credit the original author.

## 8. In-app nudges

Nudges are short, text-based prompts that point students toward help and resources: a workshop on exam stress, a wellbeing drop-in session, a finance advice clinic. They appear as a swipeable carousel on the student dashboard, prioritised by urgency.

1. In the Content Hub, open **Nudges** and select **New Nudge**.
2. Set the **Audience** (Whole University or Specific Cohort).
3. Enter a **Headline** and a short **One-line description**.
4. Choose a **Category**, which sets its priority in the feed.
5. Optionally add an **Event Date**, and use **Pin to top** for time-sensitive items.
6. Add the **Link URL** the nudge points to.
7. Select **Create Nudge**.

Your active nudges are listed so you can **Edit**, **Archive**, or reprioritise each one. When a pin date passes, content automatically returns to its normal position in the feed, with no manual intervention required.

### Targeting content to a cohort

Both articles and nudges share the same **Audience** control. Switch from **Whole University** to **Specific Cohort** and narrow with cascading dropdowns by **School**, **Department**, **Year group**, **Accommodation type**, **Hall of residence**, and **Study mode**. A live count shows how many students match as you refine the filters. Content with no targeting stays visible to everyone.

This connects directly to the cohort analytics. If you spot a wellbeing trend in a particular group, for example elevated stress in Nursing students during clinical placements, you can immediately create targeted support content that only reaches that group, without generating noise for the rest of the university.

## 9. Cohort Analysis

Cohort Analysis lets you compare how different groups of students are doing side by side, for example first years against finalists, or one hall of residence against another. It opens from **Cohort Analysis** in the left sidebar.

The groups you can build come straight from your own institution structure and profile fields, so the filters always reflect your terminology. The figures shown are aggregated and anonymised: a comparison only populates from real data once enough students in each group have checked in (the same minimum group size of five used everywhere else). Below that threshold, and in any demonstration, the charts show **illustrative sample data** so you can see how the tool behaves without exposing anyone.

### Building a cohort

1. In the **Build Cohort** panel, open any filter (for example **School**, **Department**, **Year group**, **Accommodation**, **Hall of residence**, or **Study mode**) and tick the values that define the group. Selecting more than one value within a filter widens the group; combining filters narrows it.
2. Select **Add Cohort**. The group appears under **Selected Cohorts** with its own colour.
3. Repeat to add more groups to compare (up to eight). Select any saved cohort to edit it, or use the **×** to remove it.

### Comparing and reading the chart

- **Metric**: choose what to compare: **Mind Measure Score**, **Mood Score**, **Engagement Rate**, or **Response Count**.
- **Chart Type**: **Line** and **Area** show movement over time and add a **Time Range** control (**7d**, **30d**, **90d**); **Bar** and the others compare groups at a glance.
- Each cohort keeps its colour across every chart, so you can follow a group as you change views.

Read cohort comparisons as a prompt to look closer, not a verdict. A consistent gap between two groups over time is more meaningful than a single day's difference, and a small group's figures will always be the least reliable. Use **Save to Dashboard** to keep a comparison you return to often.

> **Good to know:** because every figure is aggregated to groups of at least five, Cohort Analysis can never single out an individual. Very small groups simply will not produce a reliable comparison, which is the intended behaviour rather than a fault.

## 10. The Alerts system

Alerts are time-sensitive, university-wide messages that render as a card on every student's mobile dashboard within seconds. They are managed in the **Alerts** section of your university CMS.

> For the full technical reference (severity tiers, audience targeting, data model, and the privacy guard-rails), see [Architecture: Institutional Alerts](/architecture/institutional-alerts).

1. Open **Alerts** and select **New alert**.
2. Choose a **Severity**:
   - **Urgent**: safety. Campus closed, evacuation, shelter in place.
   - **Welfare** ("We are thinking of you"): a supportive message after distressing news. **Requires support links.**
   - **Notice** ("Heads up"): schedule change, fire alarm test, library hours.
   - **Info** ("FYI"): welcome week event, careers fair, society launch.
3. Enter a **Title** (up to 120 characters) and **Body** (up to 1000). Keep it plain and calm.
4. Set the **Audience**: **Everyone**, or **Specific students** narrowed by faculty, year, residence, or any other student attribute. The screen shows roughly how many students the alert will reach.
5. For a **Welfare** alert, add at least one **Support link** (for example, your Student Welfare team or out-of-hours service), up to three. The label and a `https://` or `tel:` destination are both required.
6. Check the **mobile preview**, select **Send now**, and confirm at the **Send this alert?** prompt.

**A built-in safeguard:** an alert cannot be sent to fewer than five matching students. If your filters narrow the audience below that, you will see **"Fewer than 5 students match"** and **Send now** stays disabled until you broaden it. This prevents an alert being effectively addressed to a single person.

<img src="/images/handbook/uni-alert-new.png" alt="The New alert dialog showing the four severity tiers, title and body fields, audience reach of 1,800 students, and a mobile preview" style={{ width: '100%', borderRadius: '8px', margin: '1rem 0' }} />

*The New alert dialog: pick a severity, write a plain title and body, set the audience (here Everyone, reaching 1,800 students), and check the live mobile preview before sending.*

Each alert keeps a **reach** card once it is live, showing how many students it was sent to, how many have **seen** it, and how many have **dismissed** it. Live alerts can be archived early.

## 11. The Conversational Insight Hub

Conversational Insight lets you pose a natural-language question that Jodie weaves into matching students' check-ins, then read the anonymised themes in their answers. A survey replacement that does not feel like a survey. It is a paid add-on; once enabled, an **Open Insight Hub** card appears in your dashboard, and the hub lives at its own address.

<img src="/images/handbook/uni-insight-hub.png" alt="The university Insight Hub home showing the most active question, response count, sentiment split, an AI-written summary, other live questions, the cohort divergence matrix, and recent quotes" style={{ width: '100%', borderRadius: '8px', margin: '1rem 0' }} />

*The Insight Hub home: your most active question with its sentiment split and an AI-written summary of what students are telling you, your other live questions, the cohort divergence matrix, and a sample of recent anonymised quotes.*

The Insight Hub sidebar offers **Home**, **Questions**, **Cohort Compare**, and **Archive**. At the top you can **Ask a new question**, **Export 30d CSV**, switch the window between **30d**, **90d**, and **1y**, and **Refresh**.

### Authoring a question

1. Select **Ask a new question**.
2. In **Question**, phrase it the way you would say it aloud, for example *"How are you finding the new assessment schedule?"* (up to 350 characters).
3. Optionally add **Internal notes** for your team. These are never shown to students or sent to Jodie.
4. Choose how often Jodie asks: **When natural** (only if the conversation invites it) or **Every check-in** (use sparingly).
5. Choose how long it runs: a set number of days, or **Until I archive it**. It auto-archives at the end; responses remain available.
6. Choose **Who should be asked**: everyone, or a specific cohort. A live count shows how many students match.
7. Select **Save draft** to hold it, or **Publish** to push it live to Jodie.

### Reading the answers

Once at least five students have answered, the Home view shows anonymised, aggregated **themes** under **What students are telling us**, an overall **sentiment** split, and a sample of paraphrased recent quotes under **Voice of the workforce**. Below that floor the hub shows **"awaiting more responses"** rather than risk identifying anyone.

The **Cohort divergence** matrix shows which groups feel differently about each question, sliced by **Faculty / School**, **Year group**, **Living situation**, or **Study mode**. Cells with fewer than five responses are redacted for privacy.

**Good to know:** each student is asked at most two custom questions per check-in, and never the same question twice. This stops cohorts being over-surveyed and keeps the conversation natural.

### The cohort insight report

From the Insight Hub you can run a **cohort insight report**: a structured, shareable summary comparing how different groups responded, with an AI-written narrative and recommended actions, ready to download as a PDF for your leadership team.

## 12. Reporting

Mind Measure produces reports for different audiences. None of them ever reveals an individual's score or conversation, and all of them respect the minimum group size of five.

| Report | Who creates it | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| **Individual wellbeing report** | The student, in the app | Their own recent history |
| **Cohort insight report** | You, from the Insight Hub | How groups answered a question |
| **Dashboard export** | You, from the dashboard | The current aggregated view |

### The individual wellbeing report

Students can generate their own downloadable wellbeing report from the mobile app, covering their recent check-in history with their score trend, themes, and a written summary. This is **theirs**: you do not trigger it and you never see it. It exists so that a student can keep a personal record or choose to share it with their GP or a counsellor.

### The cohort insight report

When you are running a Conversational Insight question, you can produce a **cohort insight report** from the Insight Hub: a structured, shareable PDF comparing how different groups responded, with an AI-written narrative and recommended actions. See [the Insight Hub](#11-the-conversational-insight-hub).

# Part C: Reference

## Platform architecture

Mind Measure is built on a cloud-native architecture designed for security, scalability, and reliability:

- **Mobile application**: a native iOS and Android app (built with Capacitor) wrapping the check-in experience
- **Institutional dashboard**: a secure web application for university administrators
- **Content Hub** and **Insight Hub**: standalone web applications for content and conversational insight
- **Backend services**: hosted on Amazon Web Services (AWS), providing enterprise-grade infrastructure
- **Database**: Amazon Aurora Serverless v2 PostgreSQL with automatic scaling, encryption, and multi-availability-zone deployment

### Infrastructure

The platform runs on AWS and Vercel:

- **Compute**: serverless functions that scale automatically with demand
- **Storage**: Amazon S3 with AES-256 encryption for all stored objects
- **AI services**: Amazon Bedrock for text analysis, Amazon Rekognition for visual analysis
- **Conversational AI**: ElevenLabs multimodal engine for natural conversation
- **Content delivery**: Amazon CloudFront CDN
- **Authentication**: Amazon Cognito with multi-factor authentication

### Security measures

| Layer | Implementation |
|---|---|
| **Encryption in transit** | TLS 1.3 for all communications |
| **Encryption at rest** | AES-256 for all stored data |
| **Authentication** | Multi-factor authentication required for all administrative access |
| **Access control** | Role-based access control with granular permissions |
| **Audit logging** | Comprehensive, tamper-proof audit trails for all data access |
| **Monitoring** | Real-time security monitoring with automated alerting |
| **Backup** | Daily automated backups with 35-day retention and point-in-time recovery |
| **Disaster recovery** | Recovery time objective under 6 hours, recovery point objective under 15 minutes |

### Compliance

Mind Measure is designed to meet the requirements of **UK GDPR** (full data subject rights), **HIPAA** (administrative, physical, and technical safeguards), and **SOC 2 Type II** (security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy).

### What data is collected

| Data type | Purpose | Retention |
|---|---|---|
| Account information (email, name) | Authentication and personalisation | Duration of account |
| University affiliation | Routing to correct institutional dashboard | Duration of account |
| Assessment responses and scores | Wellbeing tracking and trend analysis | As required by data retention policy |
| Audio data | Real-time vocal analysis only | Processed and deleted within 24 hours |
| Aggregated analytics | Institutional reporting | As required |

### What is never collected

Precise location data; contact lists or address books; photos or videos from the device (the camera is used only during live conversation, with no recordings stored); other app usage or browsing history; any data used for advertising.
