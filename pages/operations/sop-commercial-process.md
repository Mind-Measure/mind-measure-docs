# Commercial Process SOP

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** June 2026  
**Owner:** Founder / Account Management  
**Applies to:** Mind Measure Pro (corporate) and Mind Measure (university)

---

## Purpose

This SOP covers the full commercial process from first contact to signed contract. It is written for anyone running sales conversations on behalf of Mind Measure: initially the Founder, and subsequently any Account Manager hire.

---

## Scope and variants

The corporate (Pro) and university sales cycles differ in length and in who makes the decision. Where they diverge, this is noted explicitly:

- **[Pro only]** for steps that apply to corporate prospects only
- **[Uni only]** for steps that apply to university prospects only
- No marker means the step applies to both

---

## Commercial strategy

Mind Measure's current approach is to close a small number of paying institutional clients before pursuing aggressive growth or external investment. Running costs are under £1,000 per month, which means there is no pressure to accelerate before the model is proved.

The primary market is corporate. Corporate procurement decisions move faster than public sector ones, the ROI framing is familiar to HR and finance teams, and a single 5,000-person contract represents meaningful ARR. Universities are a strong secondary market with a clear regulatory driver (OfS Condition B3), but cycles are longer and procurement is more committee-driven.

**Pricing signals quality.** Mind Measure Pro is priced at the top of the market deliberately. The first marquee contract sets the reference rate for all future contracts. Never open a negotiation by moving on price. Offer concessions on scope or add-ons before touching the per-head rate.

## Ideal customer profile

### Corporate (Mind Measure Pro)

A well-qualified corporate prospect has most of the following characteristics:

- 500 or more employees (below this, unit economics are difficult)
- An active HR or People function with budget authority over wellbeing spend
- An EAP or existing wellbeing programme in place (means they understand the category; does not compete with Mind Measure)
- A named senior sponsor: HR Director, Chief People Officer, or equivalent
- A regulatory or reputational driver (financial services, professional services, healthcare, tech)
- Operating in one or more countries where English is the working language of the professional population

### Universities [Uni only]

A well-qualified university prospect has most of the following characteristics:

- UK higher education institution with a student population above 5,000
- An active student wellbeing or student services function
- Awareness of OfS Condition B3 and evidence of active work on student outcome reporting
- A named senior sponsor: Director of Student Services, Pro-Vice-Chancellor (Student Experience), or equivalent
- Previous investment in mental health or wellbeing initiatives (means budget has been allocated to the category)

## Pipeline stages

| Stage | Definition |
|---|---|
| 1. Lead | A named individual at a qualifying organisation; first contact not yet made |
| 2. First contact | Initial outreach sent or received; no conversation yet |
| 3. Discovery | First substantive conversation completed; prospect is qualified |
| 4. Demo | Product demonstrated; prospect is actively evaluating |
| 5. Proposal | Commercial proposal sent; prospect is considering |
| 6. Negotiation | Terms being discussed; close is likely |
| 7. Closed won | Contract signed; handed off to Customer Success |
| 8. Closed lost | Prospect declined or disengaged; reason recorded |

Update the pipeline record after every interaction. Never let a stage go more than two weeks without an update or a deliberate decision to pause.

## Phase 1: Lead generation and first contact

### Step 1.1 — Identify and record leads

A lead can come from several sources:

- Warm introduction via the NED or existing network
- Inbound enquiry via the website or direct email
- Conference, event, or webinar
- LinkedIn outreach

For every lead, record the following before making contact:

- Organisation name and size
- Prospect name and role
- Lead source
- Any context (how they heard about Mind Measure, what they mentioned)

Warm introductions take priority. They arrive with context, credibility, and a lowered first-contact barrier. Follow up within 24 hours.

### Step 1.2 — First contact message

The goal of first contact is a conversation, not a sale. The message should be short, specific to the prospect, and focused on what they are likely to care about.

**[Pro only]** Lead with the outcome framing: organisations do not have reliable, real-time data on workforce wellbeing. Mind Measure provides it, privately, without requiring employees to disclose anything to their employer.

**[Uni only]** Lead with the regulatory and operational framing: OfS Condition B3 requires universities to demonstrate they are supporting student outcomes. Mind Measure gives universities the population-level data to do that, and the documentation to evidence it.

Do not lead with product features. Do not attach a deck to a cold first message.

If contact is not made within five working days of the first attempt, follow up once more. If there is no response to a second attempt, mark as dormant and revisit in 90 days.

## Phase 2: Discovery

### Step 2.1 — Book the discovery call

The discovery call is 30 to 45 minutes. Its purpose is to understand the prospect's situation well enough to know whether and how Mind Measure solves a real problem for them.

Book it with the most senior person available. If the warm contact is not the budget holder, ask whether the budget holder should be on the call.

### Step 2.2 — Discovery call framework

Run the conversation in this order. Listen more than you talk.

**1. Open with their context (10 minutes)**

Ask:
- What prompted them to take the call?
- What does their current approach to wellbeing monitoring look like?
- What data do they have today on how their people are doing?
- What happens when someone is struggling?

The goal is to understand the gap before naming it. Let the prospect describe the problem in their own words.

**2. Name the problem (5 minutes)**

Reflect back what you heard and frame the Mind Measure proposition directly. Do not over-pitch. One clear sentence: "What you're describing is the problem Mind Measure is built to solve: you're making decisions about your people's wellbeing without real-time data, because the data doesn't exist yet."

**3. Explain how it works (10 minutes)**

Cover the following, in plain terms:

- What the employee experience is (a daily three to five minute AI conversation on their phone; no forms, no questionnaires after the baseline)
- What the organisation sees (anonymised, aggregated population-level trends; minimum cohort size of five; no individual data)
- Why the privacy architecture matters (employees trust it because their employer cannot see their individual results; that trust is what drives honest, consistent check-ins)
- What the baseline assessment is (PHQ-2, GAD-2, self-reported mood; establishes a starting point before the daily check-ins begin)

**4. Qualification questions (10 minutes)**

Ask:
- How many people would be in the pilot cohort?
- Who would own this internally?
- What does the procurement process look like for something like this?
- What does success look like at the end of a pilot?
- Is there a timeline in mind?

**5. Next steps (5 minutes)**

If the conversation goes well, agree the next step before hanging up. Usually a demo. Confirm the date before the call ends.

### Step 2.3 — Post-discovery notes

Within two hours of the call, record:

- Key facts (headcount, procurement process, timeline, decision-maker)
- Problem statement in the prospect's own words (useful for proposal framing later)
- Any concerns or objections raised
- Agreed next step and date
- Pipeline stage update

## Phase 3: Demo

### Step 3.1 — Prepare the demo

The demo should be tailored to what the discovery call surfaced. Before the demo:

- Confirm the audience (who is attending, what their role is)
- Review your discovery notes: what problem were they trying to solve?
- Decide which parts of the product to show based on their context

**[Pro only]** Prioritise: the employee check-in experience, the admin dashboard overview, cohort filters, the Conversational Insight Hub (if they mentioned pulse surveys or custom questions), and the hierarchy of help / EAP integration.

**[Uni only]** Prioritise: the student check-in experience, the university dashboard, the OfS Condition B3 data narrative, the Wellbeing Protection framework, and the Content Hub.

Use illustrative sample data where real institutional data is not available. The cohort comparison tool shows illustrative data below the minimum cohort size threshold, which is safe for demo purposes.

### Step 3.2 — Demo structure

Run the demo in this order:

1. Recap what you heard in discovery (one minute): "You told us X. This is how Mind Measure addresses that."
2. Employee experience: walk through a live check-in with Jodie. Show the voice interaction, the score, the reflection.
3. Institutional dashboard: show population trends, cohort filters, the anonymisation in action. Be explicit: "The individual data that generated this view is not accessible to you. You see the pattern, not the person."
4. The feature most relevant to their stated need (Conversational Insight Hub, Content Hub, Weekly Reflection, or Wellbeing Protection).
5. Leave ten minutes for questions.

Do not demo every feature. A focused demo is more effective than a comprehensive one. The goal is to produce a clear "we want to know more" reaction, not to close on the call.

### Step 3.3 — Handle the common objections

**"We already have an EAP."**
Mind Measure is not an EAP. An EAP provides support when someone asks for it. Mind Measure tells you what is happening before anyone asks. The two are complementary; Mind Measure is often how employees find their way to the EAP.

**"What about data privacy?"**
The privacy architecture is the product differentiator, not a feature to defend. Walk through the separation principle clearly: the organisation sees only anonymised population data; the minimum cohort size of five is enforced at query level; individual data is never accessible to the employer. We use this framing deliberately because it is the only way to generate honest, consistent data.

**"Is the scoring clinically validated?"**
The baseline assessment uses validated clinical instruments (PHQ-2, GAD-2). The daily check-in uses a multimodal fusion model combining text, audio, and visual signals. The model has not yet been externally validated against PHQ-9 and GAD-7 at population scale; that longitudinal study is the research priority. Be honest about this. Frame it as the difference between a clinical diagnostic tool and a continuous monitoring tool: Mind Measure surfaces patterns and trends that inform action; it does not diagnose.

**"Our employees won't use it."**
Engagement is driven by the quality of the Jodie conversation and the Weekly Reflection. Employees who feel the check-in understands them keep using it. The design deliberately avoids gamification that rewards frequency at the expense of honesty.

**"The price is too high."**
Do not drop the per-head rate. If there is a concession to make, offer it on scope: waiving the Conversational Insight Hub fee for the pilot period, or reducing the setup fee for a named launch partner. The per-head ladder is the reference rate and moving it undermines every future negotiation.

**[Uni only] "Our procurement process requires multiple sign-offs."**
Acknowledge it and use it: "We understand. Tell us who the right people are to involve and we will put together whatever you need for each audience. We can produce an executive summary for your PVC, a privacy and data summary for your DPO, and a technical overview for IT. We have done this before."

## Phase 4: Proposal

### Step 4.1 — Proposal timing

Send a proposal within three working days of a demo where the prospect expressed clear interest. Do not delay. Waiting signals low urgency.

### Step 4.2 — Proposal content

A Mind Measure proposal is not a brochure. It should be short, specific to the prospect, and structured as a decision document.

Include:

**1. Their problem, as they described it**  
One paragraph reflecting back what you heard. This shows you were listening.

**2. The proposed solution**  
What is included in the pilot: the product configuration, the onboarding support, the training, the reporting.

**3. The pilot scope**  
Named cohort size, geography, and pilot duration.

**4. Commercial terms**

For corporate prospects [Pro only]:

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| One-off setup fee | $15,000 to $25,000 (confirm based on scope) |
| Per-employee annual fee | $36 per head for 1,000 to 5,000 employees |
| | $30 per head for 5,000 to 10,000 employees |
| | $26 per head for 10,000 to 20,000 employees |

For university prospects [Uni only]:

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Annual licence | £4 per student per year |
| Conversational Insight Hub | Quoted separately on request |

**5. What success looks like**  
Proposed pilot success criteria, incorporating what the prospect said in discovery.

**6. Next steps**  
Two clear actions: what you need from them (sign-off, data processing agreement) and what you will do next (onboarding kick-off, timeline).

**7. DPA**  
Note that a standard Mind Measure Data Processing Agreement will be provided and must be signed before onboarding begins. Attach the standard DPA template or note it will follow separately.

### Step 4.3 — Follow up on the proposal

If there is no response within five working days, follow up once. Do not follow up more than twice on an unanswered proposal. If there is no response to the second follow-up, move the deal to dormant and schedule a 60-day re-engage.

## Phase 5: Negotiation and close

### Step 5.1 — Managing negotiation

Most negotiation at pilot stage is about scope and terms, not price. The most common points of negotiation are:

- Pilot duration (we propose 12 weeks minimum; below eight weeks there is not enough data to be meaningful)
- Setup fee (moveable for a named launch partner; document any concession and the reason)
- Data retention and residency (standard is UK, eu-west-2; GCC is possible, document any deviation)
- DPA terms (use the standard template; escalate any substantive changes)
- SSO timeline (not required for pilot; agree a post-launch timeline if required)

Do not negotiate on cohort minimum size (minimum of five; non-negotiable) or the privacy architecture (the separation principle is structural, not configurable).

### Step 5.2 — Legal sign-off

Any change to standard DPA terms must be reviewed before counter-signing. Do not sign a modified DPA without review.

### Step 5.3 — Contract signature

Once terms are agreed:

1. Send the final contract and DPA for signature
2. Confirm the signed copies are received and filed in the data room
3. Record the closed-won date and contract value in the pipeline

### Step 5.4 — Handoff to Customer Success

On contract signature, brief Customer Success with the following:

- Client name and primary contact
- Agreed pilot scope (headcount, business units, countries)
- Agreed launch date
- Any commercial concessions agreed (for example, setup fee reduced, Conversational Insight Hub waived)
- Notes from discovery and demo that are relevant to configuration (custom hierarchy of help, specific bespoke question requests, SSO requirement, data residency requirement)

The Customer Success team takes ownership from this point. See the Client Onboarding SOP at `/operations/sop-client-onboarding`.

---

## Phase 6: Closed lost

### Step 6.1 — Record the reason

When a prospect declines or disengages, record the reason before closing the record. The most useful categories are:

- Price
- Procurement blocked (budget not approved, competing priorities)
- Product gap (something we cannot do that they need)
- Competitor chosen (record which one)
- Timing (not now; re-engage date agreed)
- No reason given

### Step 6.2 — Post-mortem for repeated losses

If the same reason appears three or more times, raise it as a product or commercial issue. Repeated losses on price warrant a pricing review. Repeated losses on a specific product gap warrant a roadmap discussion.
